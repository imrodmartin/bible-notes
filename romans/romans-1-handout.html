Ruin · Romans 1–3 · Lesson 1

The Gospel of God

Paul's introduction, his heart, and his thesis
Romans 1:1–17
Big Idea

Before Paul addresses humanity's ruin, he establishes the gospel he is not ashamed of: rooted in promise, centered on Christ, and powerful to save everyone who believes.

I.

The Servant and His Gospel

vv. 1–7

Paul's credentials (v. 1)

  • "Servant" (doulos) — bondslave, owned by Christ Phil. 1:1; Titus 1:1; Exod. 21:5–6
  • "Called" — not self-appointed; divine initiative Acts 9:15; Gal. 1:15–16
  • "Set apart" for the gospel — one mission Acts 13:2; Gal. 1:15

The gospel's pedigree (vv. 2–4)

  • Promised beforehand through the prophets — not new, but fulfillment Gen. 3:15; Isa. 53; Luke 24:27, 44
  • According to the flesh — descended from David, the royal line 2 Sam. 7:12–16; Matt. 1:1; 2 Tim. 2:8
  • According to the Spirit — declared Son of God by the resurrection Acts 13:33; Ps. 2:7; Rom. 4:25

The goal of the gospel (vv. 5–7)

  • "Obedience of faith" — saving faith that submits Rom. 16:26; John 3:36
  • Among "all the nations" — Paul's Gentile commission Acts 9:15; 22:21; Gal. 2:7–9
  • "Loved by God" and "called as saints" 1 Cor. 1:2; Eph. 1:4–5
Teaching note: The resurrection is the Father's public vindication of the Son's identity. The gospel stands or falls here 1 Cor. 15:14, 17.
II.

Paul's Heart for Rome

vv. 8–15

Thanksgiving (v. 8)

  • Their faith "proclaimed throughout the whole world" 1 Thess. 1:8; Rom. 16:19

Prayer and longing (vv. 9–12)

  • Paul prays constantly to visit them 1 Thess. 3:10; Phil. 1:8–9
  • Aim: impart a spiritual gift to strengthen them Rom. 15:29; Acts 1:8
  • Mutual encouragement — even an apostle expects to be encouraged Rom. 15:32; 2 Cor. 1:11

Obligation (vv. 13–15)

  • A "debtor" to Greeks and barbarians, wise and foolish 1 Cor. 9:16
  • Often planned to come but hindered Rom. 15:22–24; 1 Thess. 2:18
  • "I am eager to preach the gospel to you also" Rom. 15:20
Teaching note: Paul models gospel partnership, not gospel performance. He comes to give and to receive.
III.

The Thesis: The Power of the Gospel

vv. 16–17

"I am not ashamed of the gospel" (v. 16)

  • Why it could shame: a crucified Messiah in a power-worshiping empire 1 Cor. 1:18, 23; Gal. 5:11
  • Why Paul isn't: it is "the power of God for salvation" 1 Cor. 1:24; 2:4–5
  • The Lord's own warning against shame Mark 8:38; 2 Tim. 1:8, 12

The scope (v. 16)

  • "To everyone who believes" — the condition is faith John 3:16; Rom. 10:11–13; Acts 16:31
  • "To the Jew first and also to the Greek" — priority, not favoritism Acts 13:46; Rom. 2:9–10; John 4:22

The reason (v. 17)

  • "The righteousness of God" — a righteousness from God, by faith Rom. 3:21–22; Phil. 3:9; 2 Cor. 5:21
  • "From faith to faith" — faith first to last Rom. 5:1–2; 2 Cor. 3:18
  • Anchored in Habakkuk 2:4 — "The righteous shall live by faith" Gal. 3:11; Heb. 10:38
These two verses are the thesis of the entire letter.
Everything from 1:18 onward unpacks them.

Discussion Questions

Paul calls himself a bondslave before an apostle (v. 1). What does that order teach about identity and ministry? Mark 10:43–45
How does grounding the gospel in OT promise (v. 2) strengthen our confidence in it? Luke 24:27
Paul expected mutual encouragement (v. 12). How does this reshape our view of Christian community? Heb. 10:24–25
Where are we tempted to be "ashamed of the gospel," and what in vv. 16–17 answers that? 2 Tim. 1:8
What does "the righteous shall live by faith" mean for daily living, not just initial salvation? Gal. 2:20
Takeaway

The gospel is old (promised), Christ-centered (His Son), powerful (salvation), and universal (everyone who believes). Paul is not ashamed of it, and neither should we be. Romans 1:16–17 sets the stage for all that follows: ruin, redemption, renewal, and restoration.
