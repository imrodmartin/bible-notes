Ruin · Romans 1–3 · Lesson 2

The Wrath of God Revealed

No one has an excuse — not the pagan, not the moralist
Romans 1:18–2:4
Big Idea

Having announced a righteousness from God (1:17), Paul now proves why we desperately need it. God's wrath stands revealed against all humanity — the openly wicked and the quietly self-righteous alike. Both are without excuse.

The thesis stated the cure (1:17): a righteousness received by faith. Now Paul diagnoses the disease. The same gospel that reveals righteousness (v. 17) confronts a world where God's wrath is also revealed (v. 18). Two revelations, one verb. You cannot appreciate the good news until you feel the weight of the bad.

I.

The Verdict Announced

1:18
  • "The wrath of God is revealed from heaven" — not an emotional outburst, but God's settled, holy opposition to sin Rom. 1:18; Eph. 5:6; John 3:36
  • Against "all ungodliness and unrighteousness" — sin against God (godward) and sin against man (manward)
  • Of those who "suppress the truth in unrighteousness" — the problem is not ignorance but suppression Rom. 2:8; 2 Thess. 2:10
Teaching note: Wrath is not the opposite of love; it is love's response to what destroys. A God who shrugged at evil would not be good.
II.

The Pagan Without Excuse

1:19–23

What can be known (vv. 19–20)

  • God has made Himself plain through creation — "His invisible attributes, His eternal power and divine nature" Rom. 1:19–20; Ps. 19:1–4; Acts 14:17
  • The result: people are "without excuse" (Gk. anapologetos — no defense)

The exchange (vv. 21–23)

  • They knew God but did not honor Him or give thanks Rom. 1:21
  • Claiming to be wise, they became fools 1 Cor. 1:20–21; Ps. 14:1
  • They "exchanged the glory of the immortal God" for images — idolatry is the root sin Rom. 1:23; Ps. 106:20; Jer. 2:11
III.

God Gave Them Over

1:24–32

Three times Paul says God "gave them over" (paradidomi). This is wrath in the present tense: God removes His restraint and lets sin run its course. Sin becomes its own punishment.

1 Gave them over to impurity — they exchanged the truth of God for a lie and worshiped the creature. Rom. 1:24–25
2 Gave them over to dishonorable passions — the created order itself is exchanged and inverted. Rom. 1:26–27
3 Gave them over to a debased mind — a flood of unrighteousness, and approval of those who practice it. Rom. 1:28–32
  • Note the pattern of "exchange": truth for a lie, natural for unnatural, God for self Rom. 1:25
  • The vice list (vv. 29–31) is sweeping — gossip, envy, and pride sit beside the sins we rank as "worse" Rom. 1:29–31
  • The final indictment: they not only do these things but "give approval to those who practice them" Rom. 1:32
Teaching note: "Gave them over" is not God making people sin; it is God lifting His hand of restraint. The most frightening judgment in this life can be God letting us have exactly what we insist on.
IV.

The Moralist Without Excuse

2:1–4

Chapter 2 springs a trap. The reader nodding along at chapter 1's vice list is suddenly addressed: "you have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges." The respectable moralist condemns himself by his own standard.

  • "You who judge practice the very same things" — the judge is guilty of what he condemns Rom. 2:1; Matt. 7:1–3
  • God's judgment "rightly falls on those who practice such things" — it is according to truth Rom. 2:2–3
  • To presume on God's kindness is to miss its purpose: "God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance" Rom. 2:4; 2 Pet. 3:9
"Do you presume on the riches of his kindness and forbearance and patience, not knowing that God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance?" Romans 2:4
Two kinds of people stand condemned — the immoral pagan and the moral judge.
Both need the righteousness that comes by faith.

Discussion Questions

Paul says God's wrath is already "revealed" (1:18), present tense. How does that change the way we think of wrath as only a future event?
Verse 20 says creation leaves people "without excuse." How much can someone know of God from nature alone, and where does that knowledge fall short? Ps. 19:1–4
"God gave them over" appears three times. What does it tell us about how God judges, and why is being handed over to our own desires so serious? Rom. 1:24, 26, 28
Romans 2:1 turns the spotlight on the one doing the judging. Where are we tempted to condemn in others what we excuse in ourselves? Matt. 7:3–5
If God's kindness is meant to lead to repentance (2:4), how might we be "presuming" on it instead? 2 Pet. 3:9
Takeaway

Romans 1:18–2:4 closes every mouth. The pagan suppresses the truth he sees in creation; the moralist condemns himself by his own verdict. Neither has an excuse, and both stand under God's revealed wrath. But even here grace whispers: God's kindness is still calling sinners to repent. This is the dark backdrop against which the brightness of "justified by faith" will shine.
